Hippocampus

The hippocampus plays a critical role in memory formation, spatial navigation, and motor learning. All three are extremely active during play. This is crucial to spatial memory as in mere seconds the play tracks the trajectory of the ball, and moving to an optimal position to return the shot. Lastly, determining where the shot should be placed based on the oppositions' strengths/weaknesses.

It is one of the key areas for neurogenesis(growth of new neurons). Tennis is a mentally demanding sports, which stimulates neuroplasticity requiring players to learn, adapt, and strategize in real time.

Cerebellum

The cerebellum is located at the back of the brain, plays a crucial role in coordinating movements, maintaining balance, and improving motor skills through feedback loops. It's responsible for movement coordination, Timing and rhythm, and motor learning.

The balance control for the degree of stability when going full speed to the net, and depending on the severity of the lunge to maintain the posture for an accurate follow-through. Perfecting a slice backhand by iteratively adjusting the racket angle and swing speed. Also adjusting mid-swing to account for an unexpected spin on the ball.

Motor Cortex

The motor cortex, located in the frontal lobe, is responsible for planning, controlling, and executing voluntary movements. Primary Motor Cortex: executes specific movements. Premotor Cortex: Plans and coordinates complex movements. Supplementary Motor Area: Handles movement sequences and bilateral coordination.

During repetition, motor cortex activity decreases as movements become more automated, transferring some responsibility to the cerebellum and basal ganglia. The motor cortex works with the somatosensory cortex to integrate feedback from the hand and arm to adjust grip + strength.
